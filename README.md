# $\mathrm{CO}_2$ Monitoring: A Global Analysis of the Sources of Greenhouse Gases

### Setting the Scene
I held a co-op position at Environment and Climate Change Canada under the excellent supervision Dr. Ray Nassar for 8 months from September 2020-April 2021 as part of my undergraduate Physics and Astronomy degree. This co-op focused on analyzing remote sensing data of greenhouse gases and optimizing satelitte pointing algortihms. My work with Dr. Nassar's work have been published and can be read here:

> Nassar et al. October 2023. *Intelligent Pointing Increases the Fraction of Cloud-Free CO2 and CH4 Observations from Space*. Frontiers in Remote Sensing. [Access link](https://doi.org/10.3389/frsen.2023.1233803)

At the end of my time with Dr. Nassar, I was tasked by the University with writing a report on a topic relevant to my work...

### Objectives
The project that I decided on was to analyze and compare how different hemispheres of the globe contribute to $\mathrm{CO}_2$ emissions. I decided to use observational data collected by the [Greenhouse gases Observing SATellite (GOSAT) Project](https://www.gosat.nies.go.jp/en/), which I had become familiar with over the course of my co-op term.

### Process
I analyzed the data in two ways that demonstrated skills I had learned:
1. I created .kml files, [a data type for Google Earth](https://developers.google.com/maps/documentation/earth/import-kml?platform=computer), to create a spatial map across the globe that indicated $\mathrm{CO}_2$ ppm for that individual pointing
2. I looked at the mean $\mathrm{CO}_2$ ppm for each month from 2016-2020 to determine if there were any particular monthly trends in the emission
3. I considered annual changes in $\mathrm{CO}_2$ ppm

This analysis was done for the Western, Eastern, Northern, and Southern hemispheres as split by the meridian and equitorial lines. 

### Results
I found was that my analysis of GOSAT data agreed with $\mathrm{CO}_2$ ppm reported by government agencies and climate researchers [like NOAA](https://gml.noaa.gov/ccgg/trends/). The sources of the most alarming concetrations that I found were (unsurprisingly) the USA, Europe, China, and Japan whose high concentrations were seen both in the annual averages and my spatial analayis. I also found that while the Southern hemisphere had similar mean $\mathrm{CO}_2$ ppm, they also were the lowest active emitters of greenhouse gases implying that a similar mean comes from atmospheric mixing from those larger polluters.
